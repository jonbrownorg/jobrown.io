History

            The first recorded description of the social interactions that could be enabled through networking was a series of memos written by J.C.R. Licklider of MIT in August 1962 discussing his "Galactic Network" concept. He envisioned a globally interconnected set of computers through which everyone could quickly access data and programs from any site. In spirit, the concept was very much like the Internet of today. Licklider was the first head of the computer research program at DARPA, 4 starting in October 1962. While at DARPA he convinced his successors at DARPA, Ivan Sutherland, Bob Taylor, and MIT researcher Lawrence G. Roberts, of the importance of this networking concept. Leonard Kleinrock at MIT published the first paper on packet switching theory in July 1961 and the first book on the subject in 1964.Kleinrock convinced Roberts of the theoretical feasibility of communications using packets rather than circuits, which was a major step along the path towards computer networking. The other key step was to make the computers talk together. To explore this, in 1965 working with Thomas Merrill, Roberts connected the TX-2 computer in Mass. to the Q-32 in California with a low speed dial-up telephone line creating the first (however small) wide-area computer network ever built. The result of this experiment was the realization that the time-shared computers could work well together; running programs and retrieving data as necessary on the remote machine, but that the circuit switched telephone system was totally inadequate for the job. Kleinrock's conviction of the need for packet switching was confirmed.

            In late 1966 Roberts went to DARPA to develop the computer network concept and quickly put together his plan for the "ARPANET", publishing it in 1967. At the conference where he presented the paper, there was also a paper on a packet network concept from the UK by Donald Davies and Roger Scantlebury of NPL. Scantlebury told Roberts about the NPL work as well as that of Paul Baran and others at RAND. The RAND group had written a paper on packet switching networks for secure voice in the military in 1964. It happened that the work at MIT (1961-1967), at RAND (1962-1965), and at NPL (1964-1967) had all proceeded in parallel without any of the researchers knowing about the other work. The word "packet" was adopted from the work at NPL and the proposed line speed to be used in the ARPANET design was upgraded from 2.4 kbps to 50 kbps. 5

            In August 1968, after Roberts and the DARPA funded community had refined the overall structure and specifications for the ARPANET, an RFQ was released by DARPA for the development of one of the key components, the packet switches called Interface Message Processors (IMP's). The RFQ was won in December 1968 by a group headed by Frank Heart at Bolt Beranek and Newman (BBN). As the BBN team worked on the IMP's with Bob Kahn playing a major role in the overall ARPANET architectural design, the network topology and economics were designed and optimized by Roberts working with Howard Frank and his team at Network Analysis Corporation, and the network measurement system was prepared by Kleinrock's team at UCLA. 6

            Due to Kleinrock's early development of packet switching theory and his focus on analysis, design and measurement, his Network Measurement Center at UCLA was selected to be the first node on the ARPANET. All this came together in September 1969 when BBN installed the first IMP at UCLA and the first host computer was connected. Doug Engelbart's project on "Augmentation of Human Intellect" (which included NLS, an early hypertext system) at Stanford Research Institute (SRI) provided a second node. SRI supported the Network Information Center, led by Elizabeth (Jake) Feinler and including functions such as maintaining tables of host name to address mapping as well as a directory of the RFC's. One month later, when SRI was connected to the ARPANET, the first host-to-host message was sent from Kleinrock's laboratory to SRI. Two more nodes were added at UC Santa Barbara and University of Utah. These last two nodes incorporated application visualization projects, with Glen Culler and Burton Fried at UCSB investigating methods for display of mathematical functions using storage displays to deal with the problem of refresh over the net, and Robert Taylor and Ivan Sutherland at Utah investigating methods of 3-D representations over the net. Thus, by the end of 1969, four host computers were connected together into the initial ARPANET, and the budding Internet was off the ground. Even at this early stage, it should be noted that the networking research incorporated both work on the underlying network and work on how to utilize the network. This tradition continues to this day.

             Computers were added quickly to the ARPANET during the following years, and work proceeded on completing a functionally complete Host-to-Host protocol and other network software. In December 1970 the Network Working Group (NWG) working under S. Crocker finished the initial ARPANET Host-to-Host protocol, called the Network Control Protocol (NCP). As the ARPANET sites completed implementing NCP during the period 1971-1972, the network users finally could begin to develop applications. In October 1972 Kahn organized a large, very successful demonstration of the ARPANET at the International Computer Communication Conference (ICCC). This was the first public demonstration of this new network technology to the public. It was also in 1972 that the initial

"hot" application, electronic mail, was introduced. In March Ray Tomlinson at BBN wrote the basic email message send and read software,  motivated by the need of the ARPANET developers for an easy coordination mechanism. In July, Roberts expanded its utility by writing the first emails utility program to list, selectively read, file, forward, and respond to messages. From there email took off as the largest network  application for over a decade. This was a harbinger of the kind of activity we see on the World Wide Web today, namely, the enormous growth of all kinds of "people-to-people" traffic. This,  is the basic history of the internet, and as all types of design, the design aspect has actually evolved with the internet, starting back when browsers were text only, going all the way up to the current browser which can display complex graphics and computer animation. today’s Internet is definitely complex, with the amount of things that it can do and handle but we will get into all of those aspects throughout this manual.

Understanding the Internet and HTTP

           

What is a client/server system?

A client/server system is a very keen way of distributing information across information systems like a local area network (LAN), a wide area network (WAN), or the Internet. A client/server system works something like this: A big hunk of computer (called a server) sits in some office somewhere with a bunch of files that people might want access to. This computer runs a software package (uh...also called a server unfortunately) that listens all day long to requests over the wires. The "wires" is possibly a twisted pair network hooked into a local telephone company POP or a cable or fiber optics network hooked up to a corporate WAN or LAN that is also linked up to the national telecommunications/information infrastructure through a local telephone company. Whatever the case, the specifics of the information infrastructure is beyond the scope of this tutorial, but should be mentioned. Typically, these requests will be in some language and some format the computer understands, but in English sound something like, "hello software package running on a big hunk of computer, please give me the file called "mydocument.txt" that is located in the directory "/usr/people/myname". The "server software" will then access the server hardware, find the requested file, send it back over the wires to the "client" who requested it, and then wait for another request from the same or another client. Usually, the "client" is actually a software program, like Netscape Navigator, that is being operated by a person who is the one who really wants to see the file. The client software however, deals with all the underlying client/server protocol stuff and then displays the document (that usually means interpreting HTML, but we'll get there in just a bit) to the human user. So if the web is a huge client/server system, what is the underlying client/server protocol that is used by the client software and the server software for communication? Well the client/server protocol used by the web is HTTP (Hyper Text Transport Protocol). HTTP is a protocol that is defined in several RFC´s (Request for Comments) located at the Internic and has had several generations worth of revisions (HTTP/09, HTTP/1.0 and HTTP/1.1). HTTP is a "request-response" type protocol that specifies that a client will open a connection to a server then sends a request using a very specific format. The server will then respond and close the connection.

The details of HTTP are less important for an HTML designer as they are to a web programmer, so we will not go into the specifics here (although they are available from the Illustrated Guide to HTTP referenced in the Resources section below). The main thing you need to know is that HTTP is a language spoken between your web browser (client software) and a web server (server software) so that they can communicate with each other and exchange files.

Colors and how they work

            Colors are very different in HTML because there are only so many colors that can be displayed on your monitor. In fact there are only 214 web safe colors. And what I mean by web safe is a color that shows up the same on every computer no matter what screen they have ( as long as it’s a color screen ). Now like every computer language, colors are based upon numbers. Its called the Hexadecimal System, this is the system by which all colors are deciphered by the web browser. Now with the hexadecimal system you can create any color you want in fact you can create non-web safe colors. There are only 214 web safe colors but for those who feel limited there are 256 additional non-web safe colors.  Here is how the hexadecimal coloring system works. First lets look at the term RGB this stands for Red, Green and Blue. All monitors use these three colors to create every color you see on your screen. So its only natural that HTML would use this system. Now there are 16 different shades of each Red, Blue and Green. This allows the colors to be made. This is how the shade system works. 0 represents the absence of color or white. And F represents the saturation of color wich is black. Well why not 0 – 16 well because the computer cant decipher digits longer than one character when it comes to color.

 

            So first RED, this represents the first two digits in the html color code. You will notice that each letter represents two digits. So the first two digits determine how much red is in the color you want. Green and blue are the same way,  So for example for a solid true blue color you would have no red, no green and all blue wich results in this code.

 

#0000ff = Blue

#ff0000 = Red

#00ff00 = Green

 

To create different shades of these colors then alter the other values as these will tint the color. For example if you want the color purple then you would have all blue with a little bit of red  #4400ff = Purple. Also if a color has two numbers or letters that are the same for all of the RGB values then it’s a web safe color ( #00aaff = Web safe color ). If the color has two numbers or letters that are different for one or more of the RGB values then it is not web safe ( #014590 = Not web safe). The ultimate choice is yours, you can use non-web safe colors but keep in mind they  will look differently on each computer screen. So if your design is based upon a color scheme, then stick to web safe colors.

MAKING AN  IMAGE LINKABLE

This is an easy way to remember the hardest truth in HTML code. It's called anchoring. This is the anchor tag: <a></a> This tag is very easy to use, but easy to mess up. So, let's learn how to use it properly so that we don't mess up. Let's learn how to make a link. The first two things you need to make a link is patience and a place to link to.  If you want to turn an image into a link all you need to do is take the image and put it in between the anchor tags. The code looks like this:

<a href=http://www.jonbrown.org><img  src=1.jpg></a>

  So, instead of the text becoming a link, the image, or picture, becomes the link. See how the image took the place of the text in that code? It's that simple, except for the fact that when the image shows up on your page, you will get a border around the image. If you want to eliminate the border then set the border variable in the border tag equal to 0. The code would look like this:

<a href=http://www.jonbrown.org><img  src=1.jpg border=0></a>

This is the easiest way to create an image as a link. However, there is another way to turn only a part of the image into a link.

CHAPTER THREE

WORKING WITH TABLES

 

Let's say that you have a ton of information, links, and images that you need to display on a page in a very organized way, with no hassles, that would show up symmetrically or at least somewhat uniformly on a page. You can try to use the hspace or vspace variables, but for a lot of information, that just doesn't work; in fact, it's near impossible. This is when tables come into play.

Tables are a man's best friend; move over doggies, this takes the cake. Tables display images and text in a user-friendly way, and they're pretty simple to use. Now that we have a basic understanding about tables, let's see what a table can do and how it shows up in a web browser. This is a very basic table. It can display images and text in different cells, which make up the table as a whole.

The HTML code for the table is rather simple to understand. As you get better with the

code, you will start to see more of the syntax for these tables, and you will be able to do more dynamic stuff as you go along. Some of the things you can do with a table include changing the background color of a cell, changing the cell width and height of a cell, centering something within a cell, moving something to the top or bottom of a cell, moving something to the left or right of a cell, and aligning cells to line up together. Cells make up a table, and you can have any number of cells within your table. Every table has rows and columns. Rows go left to right, and columns go top to bottom. If you have a table with one cell in the first row, and six cells in the second row, the cell in the first row will stretch across all six cells in the second row. This is a good example of how to have a "directions" header on your web page, like those of Yahoo, Netscape, and AOL.

This is a great way to organize things on your web site. This topic is so vast and wonderful that it would easily take up 20 pages to explain every variable and every aspect in detail-but I just don’t have the time. Don't get me wrong, I love this stuff, but I like sleep too.

Anyway, the table code is very easy to understand. Once you break it down and understand all of the variables for each tag, you're good to go. Let's look at the code and how it starts: This is the table tag.

 

<TABLE>  </TABLE>

 

 It has lots of variables; most of that should look familiar to you at this point. Each variable in this tag has been explained before. If you have a question about any of the variables, please don't hesitate to ask, because it's just silly to type the same thing in over and over and over again just for the sake of typing information. <TABLE WIDTH=”100%” HEIGHT=”100%”>

<TABLE WIDTH=”450” HEIGHT=”20”>

 

 They can be set to a numeric value, or a percentage value. When you set them to a percentage value, you are asking for a percentage of the screen as a whole, so to speak. When the browser window is resized, the table will move with the browser window, and keep the proportions even. This is what makes using percentages dynamic.

 

 

<TABLE WIDTH=”100%” HEIGHT=”100%” BORDER=0>

 

Border This is the border variable. This can be set to a numeric value. Remember the border is measured in points, not pixels, so when you use a border don't be alarmed. Borders in tables are different because they are beveled. This just means that they are shaded on one side and not the other. There is no way to change the color of the border unless you change the background color. Whoa, this brings us to our next thing!

 

<TABLE WIDTH=”100%” HEIGHT=”100%” BGCOLOR=”#FFFFFF”>

 

Background Color The bgcolor, or background color, variable is used to change the color of the background in your cell or table. It's like a little mini web page within the cell. You can do anything with tables; the possibilities are endless. Well, actually, they do end somewhere, I just don't know where.

Cell tags. The cell tags are what make up the cell within the tables. Now, some of you might say, "hey, that's common sense" but believe me, you will want to thank me later on. This is the cell tag.
