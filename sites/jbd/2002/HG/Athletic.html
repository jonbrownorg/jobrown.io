Scottish Heavy Events

Among the many events and competitions at a Scottish festival, the Highland Athletics, or Heavy Events, rank among the greatest crowd pleasers. There are generally seven of these tests of strength and technique; they include the Stone Putt, the 56-Pound Throw, the 28-Pound Throw, the Hammer Throw (16 or 22 pounds), the dramatic Caber Toss, the Sheaf Toss, and the 56-Pound Toss.

Despite the emphasis on individual performance, the sense of camaraderie among the competitors makes the athletics enjoyable for all. First timers, as well as seasoned veterans, receive advice and encouragement from their fellow athletes.

The Events

• Stone Putt: Using various techniques, each contestant tries to propel a 16- to 22-pound stone for the greatest distance.

• 56- and 28-Pound Stone Throws: Using one hand and spinning his body, each contestant tries to throw a weight for greatest distance.

• 16- and 22-Pound Hammer Throws: Contestants swing a lead ball on a rigid shaft to build momentum for a throw for greatest distance.

• Caber Toss: Athletes stand a 15- to 25-foot long wooden pole (weighing from 75 to 150 pounds) on end, lift it, and then try to flip it over so that the caber lands on its top end and the bottom end falls straight away from the contestant.

• Sheaf Toss: Athletes use a three-tined pitchfork to toss a 16- or 20-pound bag for height over an adjustable bar.

• 56-Pound Toss: Athletes use one hand to toss a 56-pound weight for height over an adjustable bar.

Sheepdog Demonstrations
by David Kennard
of Wellscroft Farm Chesham, New Hampshire

David Kennard of Wellscroft Farm in Chesham, New Hampshire, has been raising, training, and selling Border Collies for over 20 years. He has pleased audiences throughout New England with his informative and entertaining dog herding demonstrations at county fairs, wool days, and numerous special events. He is known for his ability to work several dogs simultaneously as he puts such animals as sheep and ducks through their routine. He always takes time to explain to the audience how and why the dog is able to move the animals. He frequently will engage children from the audience to try to put the sheep in the pen without the assistance of a dog. This proves to be very amusing and further emphasizes how one dog can do quickly what it would take many people a longer time to do.
Kennard's border collie takes a rest after a day of sheep hearding.
